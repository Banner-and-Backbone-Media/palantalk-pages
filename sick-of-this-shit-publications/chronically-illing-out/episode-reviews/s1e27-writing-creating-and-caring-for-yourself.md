# Chronically Illing Out | E27 - Writing, Creating, and Caring For Yourself

## Guest Kait Justice maps the parallel journey of living with EDS, POTS, and fibromyalgia while building an independent journalism practice—and the quiet power of self-care for chronically ill creators.

> "Without guilt, without hesitation, you are allowed this moment of quiet stillness."
>
> _\~ Kait Justice \~_

### Chronic Illness In Review

Kait Justice arrives at this episode not as an abstract thinker about chronic illness but as someone who's lived—and advocated—through it. She carries fibromyalgia, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, dysautonomia with POTS, cervical cranial instability, and a decade's worth of misdiagnosis. But the conversation Nick, Beth, Soso, and Stephanie navigate with her doesn't retreat into symptom lists. Instead, it holds the messy reality: what it means to build a life, create work, and parent children when your body is constantly recalibrating its own architecture.

The episode opens with Kait's own odyssey of discovery—years spent thinking she was lazy, unreliable, weak—only to find that her connective tissue was literally decomposing. This matters because the hosts understand it viscerally. They've all been there: the gap between what you can see and what the system refuses to believe. Kait's description of her tendons as "rotted rubber bands" that never snap back becomes a kind of emblem for the episode's core argument: chronic illness isn't just pain or fatigue. It's your body operating without structural scaffolding, your muscles compensating endlessly, your mind forced to become a kind of medical detective just to function.

What emerges from this hour is a portrait of illness not as tragedy but as an identity that shapes everything—and therefore must be managed with the same intensity you'd bring to a career, a relationship, or a home. Kait has learned to advocate for her daughter; she's learned to ask doctors educated questions instead of accepting dismissals; she's learned that sometimes TikTok, not medical training, delivers diagnosis. She's also learned something harder: that self-knowledge matters more than professional credentials when those professionals have god complexes and 15 minutes of attention to spend. The medical system has failed her. But because it failed her, she built her own framework for survival.

The conversation around EDS particularly cuts deep. The hosts track how Kait's diagnosis shifted everything—not just treatment but identity, parenting, the way she understands her past. Why was she always the kid who couldn't do what everyone else could? Because her joints don't stay in place. Why does her daughter's kneecap dislocate at school and send her to the hospital? Because she inherited the same faulty connective tissue. But Kait got early intervention for her daughter, which means her daughter might avoid the cascading pain Kait now experiences in her late thirties. Knowledge gained through suffering, deployed to protect the next generation—that's the unspoken bargain Kait has made with her own illness.

The final segment pivots into something quieter and more profound. Kait reads a meditation she's written—a text that reframes self-care not as spa days or breathing exercises, but as essential medical practice. "Without guilt, without hesitation, you are allowed this moment of quiet stillness." She positions rest alongside resistance, meditation alongside accountability. For chronically ill creators, this isn't luxury. It's the difference between sustaining a practice and burning out entirely. Her Substack, Downwind of Truth, exists because she's learned to protect her capacity to create. It's investigative journalism about government corruption and corporate accountability—substantive work—published freely, supported by readers who understand that good information requires sustainable creators.

This episode insists on a truth the medical system keeps trying to deny: people with chronic illness are already experts. They're already surviving incredible odds. The question isn't whether they're competent enough to understand their own bodies. It's whether we'll build systems that trust them, support them, and let them create.

\~ Nick Paro
